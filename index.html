23 JUN 2018 | Pros and Cons of: Navy Life

One of the things I am most greatful to the Navy for is the simplifications it has made to my daily life and other Quality of Life improvements. So here are the PROS OF NAVY LIFE:

  1. NO NEED TO THINK ABOUT TEXT FORMATTING. ALWAYS USE MONOSPACED FONTS WITH SUBSCRIPTS AND SUPERSCRIPTS OF VARYING WIDTH FOR READABILITY AND ALL CAPITALS LETTERS ARE GOOD AND SENTANCES.
  2. Most people spend their whole lives breathing standard atmospheric air (or even worse—mile-high Colorado air) all with the depressing volumetric composition 78.09% Nitrogen, 20.95% Oxygen, 0.93% Argon,...etc. But at 650++ ft below the sea the air is far richer, with over 101% net composition. And that's a fact.
  3. Logic systems are simplified by redefining the double right implication arrow (⇒) to mean equivalent to, but in the STBD direction, and without regard to the arrow of time. This allows you to do lots. For example it becomes possible to prove, analytically, that
    • Steaming flank speed into an underwater mountain ⇒ Leadership problems
    • Death ⇒ Poor Safety.
    • Result of input due to negative feedback loop ⇒ input to negative feedback loop.
    • Proposition X ⇒ All hail Admiral Rickover Lets Learn About Nuetrons!
  4. Learning extremely classified methods for clean decision making, including the Guess and Check method.
  5. Another method for decision making, most famously used to decide
    • whether we should use heavy lead shielding or lead coolants in our reactors,
    • whether we should create a crisis by putting nuclear warheads on submergable, undetectable boats xor on shifty, difficult to locate islands,
    • whether we should use binary or ternary computers, and even
    • whether we should invade a landlocked country in the middle of Asia
    is the ``Because we're not Communists.'' motivating method. Should I clean my room tonight? No—I mean, we're not communists!
  6. The Navy officially reaffirmed my previous suspicion that hexagons have six sides, that octagons cover your head, and that Pentagons manage 5 branches, depending on how independent you consider the Coast Guard and Marine Corps.
  7. Have to many forces in your life? You can use a force-taker to take forces, one of the many difficult to remember services available to service members. But don't tell me about it if you dont want me to tell.

But enough rantbling and onto the CONS OF NAVY LIFE:

  1. "Ribs" every thursday.
  2. Having to learn a new dictionary of terms, including:
    • Excellence: a quantitative quality measured by how much time you spend in the Rickover.
    • Integrity: a quantitative quality measured by how much time you spend in the Rickover.
    • FCEONRASW130OB: Future Competance at engineering and operating a nuclear reactor at sea with 130 sailors on board—a qualitative value measured by how much time you spend in the Rickover.
  3. Using the head while brown tank is being discharged with [≥ discharge depth pressurized air].
  4. Cross connecting san. tanks while brown tank is being discharged with [≥ discharge depth pressurized air].
  5. Playing king of the sonar dome when brown tank is being discharged with [≥ discharge depth pressurized air].
  6. Being the sacrificial Junior Officer during san. tank or bilge maintenance.
  7. When Navy logic system doesn't help you decide what to do when attacked by a polar bear. USS Conneticut Wikipedia
