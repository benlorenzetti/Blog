23 JUN 2018 | Pros and Cons of: Navy Life

One of the things I am most greatful to the Navy for is the simplifications it has made to my daily life and other Quality of Life improvements. So here are the PROS OF NAVY LIFE:

  1. NO NEED TO THINK ABOUT TEXT FORMATTING. ALWAYS USE MONOSPACED FONTS WITH SUBSCRIPTS AND SUPERSCRIPTS OF VARYING WIDTH FOR READABILITY AND ALL CAPITALS LETTERS ARE GOOD AND SENTANCES.
  2. Logic systems are simplified by redefining the right implication arrow (⇒) to mean equivalent to, but in the STBD direction, and without regard to the arrow of time. This allows you to do lots. For example it becomes possible to prove, analytically, that
    • Death ⇒ Poor Safety.
    • Result of input A due to negative feedback loop ⇒ input A to negative feedback loop.
    • Proposition X ⇒ All hail Admiral Rickover Lets Learn About Neutrons!
  3. Learning highly classified methods for problem solving, including the Guess and Check method.
  4. Another method for decision making, is the ``Because we're not Communists.'' motivating method. Should I clean my room tonight? No—I mean, we're not communists!
  5. The Navy officially reaffirmed my previous suspicion that hexagons have six sides, that octagons cover your head, and that Pentagons manage 5 branches, depending on how independent you consider the Coast Guard and Marine Corps.
  6. Have to many forces in your life? You can use a force-taker to take forces, one of the many difficult to remember services available to service members. But can the force-taker be compelled to reveal confidential communications?

But enough rantbling and onto the CONS OF NAVY LIFE:

  1. "Ribs" every thursday.
  2. Having to learn a new dictionary of terms, including:
    • Excellence: a quantitative quality measured by how much time you spend in the Rickover.
    • Integrity: a quantitative quality measured by how much time you spend in the Rickover.
    • Knowledge: I can't remember this one actually.
  3. Playing king of the sonar dome when brown tank is discharged.
  4. Being the sacrificial Junior Officer during bilge maintenance.
  5. When Navy logic system doesn't help you decide what to do when attacked by a polar bear. USS Conneticut Wikipedia
