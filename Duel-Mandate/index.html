Duel Mandate: The Irony and Inequity of the Federal Reserve System

Have recent years provided any natural experiments for the various schools of macroeconomics: New-Keynesianism, Monetarism, or Real Business Cycle theory?

  2. Inflation Inertia (Long Live the Phillips Curve)

This article is a Monetarist critique of the policies and dogma of the Federal Reserve and new-Keynesian economics. It is also a positive prediction of future trends, especially that wealth inequality will peak.

1. The Phillips Curve is Dead

The Phillips curve is the inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment, an academic observation on which the Federal Reserve's mandate is partially based. The plot below shows the inverse relationship from the US in the 1960s. The idea is the Federal Reserve can, to some extent, lower unemployment by raising inflation.

However the Phillips curve's primacy as a policy tool has been reduced since the 1970s due to the relationship becoming inconsistent and due to a theoretical critique by Friedman and Phelps that inflation expectations (Krugman), not just unemployment, drive actual inflation.

After the 1960s, no consistent trend appears in a simple CPI-unemployment scatterplot; in the darker points (months) from the 1970s-1990s it looks much more like a random cycles than a curve. Additionally, in the 2000s (copper points) the Phillips curve appeared to flatten (Engemann) as inflation stayed low.

The concern is two-fold, can a flat Phillips curve still be used for adjusting the unemployment rate in recessions, and have any other mistakes—besides missing inflation expectations in the 1970s, as critiqued by Friedman—resulted in harmful or counterproductive Federal policy? To keep the dogma alive, research has focused on quantifying inflation expectations and isolating the effects of expectations.

This new-Keynesian, expectation-augmented Phillips curve is presented well in this talk on Is the Philips Curve Getting Flatter (Nakamura). In the equation π is actual inflation rate, Eπ the expected inflation, and -κu basically the old Keynesian Phillips curve (a slope × unemployment rate).

In the linked presentation and much contemporary literature, graphs like the one below are very popular, an idea that inflation expectations are self-fulfilling: if the Federal Reserve can convince us of its competance at achieving a certain inflation rate, that in and of itself will help make it true. This seems possible but also a very political idea. Here is their graph:

This self-fulfilling "anchoring" of inflation expectations shares a common theme with most Keynesian theories, that the people produce psychological, positive feedback loops that must be actively managed by a competent authority, in this case for inflation. For example, consider Chairman Bernanke's speech on anchoring low inflation expectations in 2007:

...Notably, the sharp increases in energy prices over the past few years have not led either to persistent inflation or to a recession, in contrast (for example) to the U.S. experience of the 1970s.

Various factors might account for these changes in the Phillips curve, but, as Mishkin pointed out, better-anchored inflation expectations—themselves, of course, the product of monetary policies that brought inflation down and have kept it relatively stable--certainly play some role.

Unlucky timing for that speech...but perhaps true.

2. Inflation Inertia (Long Live the Phillips Curve)

Many have been surprised by the apparent success of "anchored" low inflation expectations, (Sanchez and Kim) with inflation even persisiting below the Federal Reserves's target rate of 2% (why 2%). However this "anchor" was actually forecastable with a simple monetary-based rule.

In Why Do We Think that Inflation Expectations Matter for Inflation?, Mr. Rudd finds that, if "the 'true' inflation equation has a role for short-run expected inflation" and assuming a particular inflation forcasting rule, then: "the expectation of long-run inflation in this sort of world will likely be self-fullfilling: with a zero unemployment gap and no supply shocks, actual average inflation will equal the long-run mean obtained from the inflation forcasting rule."

Take the Monetarist tautology money supply (M), times "velocity of money" (V), equals the average price level (P) of goods and services, times the number of transactions (T) in the economy, where velocity is defined in terms of the other variables but intuitively is sort of like the size of your rainy day fund in the bank.

Next, assume velocity remains constant and then derive an equation for expected change in average price levels.

This is not very useful, but further assuming the number of transactions in the economy scales only with population size, i.e. everyone continues to conduct the same distribution of transactions in size and quantity, then several substitutions can be made to rewrite this equation in terms for which there is publically available data. Let Eπ be the monetary inflation rate, Qrt the population growth rate, and Gdp gross domestic product.

Thus with two assumptions, expected inflation is a function of the quantity of money (M) and population growth rate (Qrt). Although neither assumuption of constant velocity nor [T ∝ population] is necessarily true, the errors produced by both are at least bounded to a particular data point period because the equation is based on a derivative; not integration of inflation (or errors) over time.

Somewhat more difficult is choosing an appropriate measure of the money supply, which has been the main critique of Monetarism.

However, if we narrowly define the money supply as being the expected long-term money supply, then even with an independent Federal Reserve, the Federal Government is still a source of "printed money" by the Federal Debt because the government, unlike people, does not age and will never have to pay off its total debt load as long as potential tax revenue scales somewhat proportionally.

Since this narrow definition of the long term money supply is based on the number of dollars available to the people of the country, less those that will be unavailable in the future, it must also be adjusted for accumulated trade surpluses and deficits.

How well does this long term money supply and its predicted long term inflation fit the recent experience of the United States?

At first glance, poorly. There are multiple times where actual inflation leads predicted inflation in the 1970s and 1980s and the predictions of the 1990s and 2000s appear wildly off. But after 2013 the prediction becomes accurate.

This regression to predicted monetary inflation in 2013 can be explained by a series of real supply shocks having finally working their way through the economy, in particular leaving the gold standard (1971), oil shocks (1973, 1979, 1990), billateral trade with China (1979), NAFTA (1994), other trade agreements, and the computer revolution.

If this were true, one would expect a steep Phillips curve (slope -κ) during the period of supply shocks, as inflation helped distribute real price changes in the economy, and a flat curve after employment prices reached a new equilibrium with goods and services.

The graph below plots a CPI new-Keynesian Phillips curve, but with expected inflation for the period (Eπ) calculated from the long term expected monetary inflation described above instead of professional prediction surveys. Instead of plotting a output gap, it plots an inflation gap (Δπ = CPI - Eπ).

Indeed, comparing the simple CPI scatterplot from above vs. the CPI and forecasted long-term inflation gap produces a steep, relatively stable correlation between inflation and unemployment during periods attributable to supply shocks and a dramatic flattening after 2013.

The Phillips curve is dead, long live the Phillips curve! Monetarism is dead (DeLong), long live Monetarism!

3. Nominal Interest Rates and the Liquidity Trap

New-Keynesian economics is supposed to be countercyclical; the point of having a bureaucrat in charge is to dampen business cylces, making recessions less severe and expansions less speculative. At this point in America we have come to expect when a recession begins, the central bank will lower interest rates to encourage more loans and startup more economic investment and construction.

After 50 years off the gold standard, this central theory remains scientifically true—but in the sense that it has never fully been tested. The graph below shows that over all six of the last six cycles, and especially the last four, the Federal Reserve never succeeded in raising interest rates to their previous level during the good times.

Consequently, since the Great Recession interest rates have been at or near the "zero lower bound" (ZLB), which restricts the Federal Reserve's ability to further lower interest rates during future crises. The Fed cannot lower the federal funds rate below zero in the absense of reserve requirements, which ironically the Fed abolished on nonpersonal time and savings deposits (Feinman) in 1990.

New-Keynesian economists call this a liquidity trap, as defined by Keynes himself:

There is the possibility...that, after the rate of interest has fallen to a certain level, liquidity-preference may become virtually absolute in the sense that almost everyone prefers cash to holding a debt which yields so low a rate of interest. In this event the monetary authority would have lost effective control over the rate of interest. But whilst this limiting case might become practically important in future, I know of no example of it hitherto.

I.e. negative interest rates don't work very well for the Fed because every bank would simply borrow as much as it could, stash the cash in a vault, and wait to make a profit when the loan is due because less is required to pay it off.

4. Real Interest Rates and Secular Stagnation

Real interest rates, r, are defined by the Fisher equation, where i is the nominal interest rate for banks to borrow money from the Fed and π is inflation.

A second monetarist critique of "anchored" inflation expectations, is a simple assertion: a currency debased from physical commodities, like gold, but re-based on an abstract commodity, natural numbers, will have a natural real interest rate of zero. Compare the graph used to make the case for inflation expectations with a graph of nominal interest rates:

The same initial insight of inflation expectations, that inflation follows expectations, also appears true of a graph of inflation following interest rates! As we have seen, the Federal Reserve has consistently failed to restore nominal interest rates to previous levels during times of economic expansion. If the natural real interest rate is zero, then the Federal Reserve's policies alone would account for the low inflation (r = 0 ⇒ π = i). Ironically stable prices!

However, new-Keynesians would counter this assertion with two arguments:

  1. Taylor rule: central banks tend to raise interest rates to counter inflation, or lower interest rates to counter recessions (which generally have low observed inflation), so the correlation between interest rates and inflation is caused by central bank policy, not an inevitable result.
  2. Secular Stagnation: natural real interest rates are not constant and have been declining for at least four decades, for a variety of undesirable factors.

With regard to the Taylor rule, see the critique of new-Keynesian inflation expectations regarding the Phillips curve above. It would be ironic if the bureaucrats at the Fed were actually creating their own positive feedback loop on inflation.

Secular Stagnation, as used by economist today, describes the long term fall in real interest rates and its potential causes and effects, as discussed in this paper by Lukasz Rachel and Lawrence Summers. The following graph introduces their argument:

To combat secular stagnation, Rachel and Summers argue for larger budget deficits and increased fiscal programs such as pay-as-you-go old age pensions (Social Security) and government health care programs to raise neutral real rates. The reason why we should combat secular "stagnation" is considered obvious, but an actual reason why is not given. Laubach and Williams, of the LW r* model, suggest:

Since the start of the Great Recession, the estimated natural rate of interest fell sharply and shows no sign of recovering. These results are robust to alternative model specifications. If the natural rate remains low, future episodes of hitting the zero lower bound are likely to be frequent and long-lasting.

If the motivation is to avoid the zero lower bound for nominal interest rates, then there is some irony and insanity here: the "post-Keynesian" solution to activist monetary policy becoming ineffective due to overuse of loose rates over time, is persistently looser fiscal policy of deficits for decades?

It is also ironic for those of gold-bug disposition that formally leaving the gold standard gave the Federal Reserve enough independent power lower interest rates to the limit, thus rendering their powers impotent.

Finally, there is some empirical evidence for the assertion that the natural real interest rate is zero, and that this should be considered a technological/civic advancement in its own right. 5. Interest Rates and Inequality

This criticism of the Federal Reserve's dogma for inflation and interest rates has relied on two old Monetary equations, the quantity theory of money (MV = PT) and the Fisher equation (r = i - π), and the evidence provided was based on on derivative data, i.e. looking at percent changes measured between specific points in time. This had the advantage of removing cumulative creep of errors over time. However a third equation is needed to help explain the wealth effect of all of these exponential phenomenon: the geometric series (Rn) from the mortgage calculator:

For more information, see the mortgage calculator series for assessing real estate prices, which found two key results:

  1. Real estate prices are directly related to, and can be predicted by, 30-year mortgage interest rates.
  2. There is a long term trend of equity prices (S&P 500) trending in parallel with these interest rate based prices.

Both of these can be seen from graphs of real estate price to rent (P/R) ratios and stock/real estate price to earnings (P/E) ratios, although there are business cycles variations in both.

In the P/R graph, the modelled P/R ratio is driven primarily by mortgage interest rates (entirely in the graph above due to some assumptions based on data availability), suggesting that the Federal Funds Rate and other less conventional interest rate operations (QE) are almost entirely responsible for the rise in home prices since at least the 1990s.

In the P/E graph, the same modelled curve based on interest rates is plotted in yellow, except assuming full equity such that what would be a mortgage payment is instead earnings for a landlord. The red curve is a more complex model showing the use of mortgage debt increases net earnings once equity buildup is accounted for.

Scaling asset prices shown by the S&P 500 does not have a first-order impact on the Gini coefficient for wealth inequality because everyones' net worth is scaled proportionally, however there would be accumulating effects over time as labor income is able to afford fewer and fewer shares of capital stock.

6. The Dual Mandate

The Constitution says the Congress shall have power:

  • To borrow money on the credit of the United States;
  • To coin money, regulate the value thereof, and of foreign coin, and fix the standard of weights and measures;
and that:
  • No money shall be drawn from the Treasury, but in consequence of appropriations made by law; and a regular statement and account of the receipts and expenditures of all public money shall be published from time to time.
  • No state shall enter into any treaty, alliance, or confederation; grantletters of marque and reprisal; coin money; emit bills of credit; make anything but gold and silver coin a tender in payment of debts; pass any bill of attainder, ex post facto law, or law impairing the obligation of contracts, or grant any Title of Nobility.

The gold standard was broken by executive order by FDR in 1933 and ended by Nixon in 1971 (a bipartisan affair). At each instance, Congress and the Surpreme Court conceded to the executive these changes. One can argue this breaking-change leadership was necessary given the circumstances or that it was an inevitable result of advances in economics, as Keynesians or Monetarists might, but the spirit of the Constitution, where monetary policy was subject to more fundamental constraints than the whims of bureaucrats, was never properly ammended.

The current mandate of the Federal Reserve from Congress directs:

The Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System and the Federal Open Market Committee shall maintain long run growth of the monetary and credit aggregates commensurate with the economy's long run potential to increase production, so as to promote effectively the goals of maximum employment, stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates.

Ironically, this tripartite instruction has come to be known as the dual mandate, i.e. stable prices and maximum sustainable employment.

This faux pas is likely due to the prominance of the Phillips curve and IS-LM model in macro economics and their predictions of tradeoffs between inflation and unemployment (Phillips curve) and GDP and interest (LM Curve). Interest rates are seen as a policy variable to adjust these relationships, but virtually ignored is the correlation between long-term interest rates and inequality.
